## Central Concepts of Economics

---

## a) Scarcity (অপ্রতুলতা)

Scarcity means **resources are limited**, but **human wants are unlimited**.

We don’t have enough land, money, time, labor, or materials to satisfy everyone’s wants.

👉 So we must **choose**.

Because of scarcity, every country must answer 3 important questions:

1. **What to produce?**
    
2. **How to produce?**
    
3. **For whom to produce?**
    

### Simple Example:

- If a country has limited steel, should it make cars 🚗 or bridges 🌉?
    
- In Bangladesh, land is limited. Should it grow rice 🌾 or build factories 🏭?
    
- During COVID-19, should factories make ventilators or normal goods?
    

Since we can’t have everything, we must **prioritize**.

👉 Scarcity forces us to make **trade-offs** (choosing one thing and giving up another).

---

## b) Efficiency (দক্ষতা)

Efficiency means **using limited resources in the best possible way**.

There are two types:

### 1️⃣ Productive Efficiency

Producing goods at the **lowest possible cost**.  
No waste of resources.

Example:  
If a factory can produce 100 shirts using less electricity and labor, it is being efficient.

👉 No waste = Productive efficiency

---

### 2️⃣` Allocative `Efficiency

Producing the **right goods that people really want**.

Example:  
If people need food more than luxury cars, resources should be used to produce more food.

👉 Right goods for society = Allocative efficiency

---

## Very Simple Summary

Scarcity = Limited resources, unlimited wants → Must choose.  
Efficiency = Use resources wisely without waste.

------


Every economic system (any country) must solve **three basic problems** because resources are limited.

---

## 1️⃣ What to Produce?

This means:

👉 **Which goods and services should be made?**  
👉 **How much of each should be made?**

Since resources are limited, a country must decide:

- More food 🍚 or more cars 🚗?
    
- Consumer goods (food, clothes)  
    OR
    
- Capital goods (machines, factories)?
    

### Example:

If a country uses more resources to build factories, it will produce more goods in the future.  
If it produces more food and clothes now, people will live more comfortably today.

So, society must **choose**.

---

## 2️⃣ How to Produce?

This means:

👉 **What method should be used to produce goods?**

There are two main methods:

### 🔹 Labor-Intensive Method

Uses more workers and less machines.

Example:  
A garment factory hiring many workers.

### 🔹 Capital-Intensive Method

Uses more machines and fewer workers.

Example:  
A modern factory using robots and automated machines.

A country like Bangladesh often uses more labor-intensive methods because labor is abundant and cheaper.

So, the country must decide:

- Use more workers?
    
- Or use more machines?
    

---

## 3️⃣ For Whom to Produce?

This means:

👉 **Who will get the goods and services?**

Since we cannot produce enough for everyone, we must decide:

- Will goods go to rich people?
    
- Will goods go to poor people?
    
- How will income be distributed?
    

Example:  
Luxury cars are produced mainly for rich people, while basic food is for everyone.

---

## Very Simple Summary

Because of scarcity, every country must decide:

1. **What to produce** (Which goods?)
    
2. **How to produce** (Which method?)
    
3. **For whom to produce** (Who gets them?)
    

These are called the **three fundamental economic problems**.

--------

To produce goods and services, we need **resources**.  
These resources are called **Factors of Production**.

There are **4 factors of production**:

---

## 1️⃣ Land 🌍

Land means **all natural resources** — not just soil.

It includes:

- Land (in normal meaning)
    
- Minerals ⛏️
    
- Oil & gas
    
- Coal
    
- Water 💧
    
- Forests 🌳
    
- Fish 🐟
    
- Air
    

👉 In simple words:  
**Land = Gifts of nature**

Example:  
Rice needs land and water. Oil comes from natural resources.

---

## 2️⃣ Labor 👷‍♂️

Labor means **human work** — both physical and mental.

It includes:

- Farmers 🌾
    
- Factory workers 🏭
    
- Teachers 👩‍🏫
    
- Doctors 👨‍⚕️
    
- Office workers 💼
    

👉 In simple words:  
**Labor = Human effort (body + brain)**

## 3️⃣ Capital 🏗️

Capital means **man-made tools used for production**.

It includes:

- Machines
    
- Tools
    
- Buildings
    
- Factories
    
- Equipment
    

⚠️ Important:  
Capital does NOT mean money.  
Money is used to buy capital, but money itself is not capital in economics.

👉 In simple words:  
**Capital = Tools and machines used to make goods**

---

## 4️⃣ Entrepreneurship 💡

Entrepreneurship means the **person who organizes everything**.

An entrepreneur:

- Combines land, labor, and capital
    
- Makes business decisions
    
- Takes risks
    
- Introduces new ideas
    

Example:  
A business owner who starts a factory and decides what to produce.

👉 In simple words:  
**Entrepreneur = The boss who organizes and takes risks**

---

## Very Short Summary

Land = Nature 🌍  
Labor = Human work 👷  
Capital = Tools & machines 🏭  
Entrepreneurship = Organizer & risk-taker 💡

These four together help produce all goods and services in an economy.

## Income from Factors of Production

People earn money by selling the services of the factors they own.

Each factor of production has its own type of income:

1️⃣ **Land → Rent**  
If you own land and someone uses it, you earn **rent**.

Example:  
A farmer pays rent to use someone’s land.

---

2️⃣ **Labor → Wages**  
If you work for someone, you earn **wages (salary)**.

Example:  
A teacher, doctor, or factory worker gets wages.

---

3️⃣ **Capital → Interest**  
If you lend money or provide machines/tools, you earn **interest**.

Example:  
If you deposit money in a bank, the bank gives you interest.

---

4️⃣ **Entrepreneurship → Profit**  
If you start and run a business, you earn **profit**.

But remember:  
Entrepreneurs also take risks. If the business fails, they may face loss.

---

### Simple Summary

Land earns → Rent  
Labor earns → Wages  
Capital earns → Interest  
Entrepreneur earns → Profit

### 2️⃣ Based on Income & Purchasing Power

People with less income can buy fewer goods.

In most market economies, goods are distributed based on **income and purchasing power**.

Example:

- Rich people can buy luxury cars 🚗
    
- Poor people may only afford basic goods 🍚

👉 Who has the ability to pay?

# Types of Economic Systems

An economic system is **how a country decides**:

- What to produce
    
- How to produce
    
- For whom to produce
    

There are 3 main types:

---

## 1️⃣ Market Economy

In a market economy:

- Decisions are made by **individuals and businesses**
    
- Government has **very little control**
    
- Prices are decided by **demand and supply**
    

👉 Prices act like signals.  
If a product is scarce, price goes up.  
If many people want it, firms produce more.

### ✅ Advantage:

- Encourages innovation
    
- Promotes efficiency
    
- Businesses compete and improve quality
    

### ❌ Disadvantage:

- Can create inequality
    
- May cause market failures
    

### Example:

United States (USA)

---

## 2️⃣ Command Economy

In a command economy:

- Government makes **all major decisions**
    
- Central authority controls production
    
- No free market pricing
    

Government decides:

- What to produce
    
- How much to produce
    
- Who will receive goods
    

### ✅ Advantage:

- Can quickly mobilize resources
    
- Focus on national goals
    

### ❌ Disadvantage:

- Often inefficient
    
- Less innovation
    
- May ignore consumer needs
    

### Examples:

North Korea  
Soviet Union (USSR)

---

## 3️⃣ Mixed Economy

Mixed economy = Combination of market + government control.

- Private businesses operate
    
- Government also intervenes in important sectors
    

Most countries today follow this system.

### Example:

Bangladesh

Bangladesh is mostly market-based, but:

- Government gives fertilizer subsidies
    
- Controls some rice prices
    
- Regulates key industries
    

---

## Very Simple Summary

Market Economy → People & businesses decide.  
Command Economy → Government decides.  
Mixed Economy → Both government & market decide.

# Six Main Factors That Change Demand

Demand means **how much people are willing and able to buy**.

Demand changes when these 6 things change:

---

## 1️⃣ Prices of Related Goods

There are two types:

### 🔹 Substitute Goods

Goods that can replace each other.

Example: Tea ☕ and coffee ☕

If tea price rises, people may buy more coffee.

👉 Demand for coffee increases.

---

### 🔹 Complementary Goods

Goods used together.

Example: Car 🚗 and petrol ⛽

If petrol price rises, people may use cars less.

👉 Demand for cars decreases.

---

## 2️⃣ Expected Future Prices

If people think the price will rise in the future,  
they buy more now.

Example:  
If people expect rice price to increase next month,  
they will buy more today.

👉 Demand increases now.

---

## 3️⃣ Income

When income changes, demand changes.

### 🔹 Normal Goods

When income increases → demand increases.

Example: Clothes, electronics.

### 🔹 Inferior Goods

When income increases → demand decreases.

Example: Cheap-quality goods.

---

## 4️⃣ Expected Future Income & Credit

If people expect:

- Higher salary in future
    
- Easy bank loans
    

They will buy more today.

👉 Demand increases.

If they fear job loss, demand decreases.

---

## 5️⃣ Population

If population increases:

- More people
    
- More buyers
    

👉 Demand increases.

Example: In a country like Bangladesh, high population increases demand for food and housing.

---

## 6️⃣ Preferences (Tastes & Fashion)

If people like something more, demand increases.

Example:  
If a new phone becomes trendy, more people want it.

If health awareness increases, demand for junk food may fall.

---

# Very Short Summary

Demand changes because of:

1. Prices of related goods
    
2. Expected future prices
    
3. Income
    
4. Expected future income & credit
    
5. Population
    
6. Preferences

# 📘 Demand

**Demand** refers to the quantity of a good or service that consumers are **willing and able to buy** at different prices during a given period.

## 🔹 Elements of Demand

1. **Desire for a Good**
    
    - The consumer must want the product.
        
2. **Ability to Pay**
    
    - The consumer must have enough income or purchasing power.
        
3. **Willingness to Pay**
    
    - The consumer must be ready to spend money on the product.
        
4. **Price of the Good**
    
    - According to the **Law of Demand**, price and quantity demanded have an inverse relationship.
        
5. **Time Period**
    
    - Demand is measured over a specific time frame (per day, month, year, etc.).
        

---

## 🔹 Factors Affecting Demand (Determinants)

- Price of the good
    
- Consumer income
    
- Prices of related goods (substitutes & complements)
    
- Tastes and preferences
    
- Expectations about future prices
    
- Number of buyers
    

---

# 📗 Supply

**Supply** refers to the quantity of a good or service that producers are **willing and able to sell** at different prices during a given period.

## 🔹 Elements of Supply

1. **Willingness to Sell**
    
    - The producer must be ready to offer the product.
        
2. **Ability to Produce**
    
    - The producer must have the resources and technology.
        
3. **Price of the Good**
    
    - According to the **Law of Supply**, price and quantity supplied have a direct relationship.
        
4. **Time Period**
    
    - Supply is measured over a specific period.
        

---

## 🔹 Factors Affecting Supply (Determinants)

- Price of the good
    
- Cost of production
    
- Technology
    
- Government policies (taxes & subsidies)
    
- Prices of related goods
    
- Expectations
    
- Number of sellers
    

---

# 📊 Key Difference

|Demand|Supply|
|---|---|
|Comes from consumers|Comes from producers|
|Inverse relationship with price|Direct relationship with price|
|Affected by income & preferences|Affected by production costs & technology|

---

# 📉 Demand Curve

A **demand curve** is a graphical representation of the relationship between **price** and **quantity demanded**.

## 🔹 Law of Demand

As **price increases**, **quantity demanded decreases**, and vice versa (other things remaining constant).

## 🔹 Diagram of Demand Curve

```
Price (P)
  |
  |\
  | \
  |  \
  |   \
  |    \
  |_____ \________ Quantity (Q)
```

### ✔ Why is it downward sloping?

- Law of diminishing marginal utility
    
- Income effect
    
- Substitution effect
    

---

# 📈 Supply Curve

A **supply curve** shows the relationship between **price** and **quantity supplied**.

## 🔹 Law of Supply

As **price increases**, **quantity supplied increases**, and vice versa (other things remaining constant).

## 🔹 Diagram of Supply Curve

```
Price (P)
  |
  |     /
  |    /
  |   /
  |  /
  | /
  |/___________ Quantity (Q)
```

### ✔ Why is it upward sloping?

- Higher price = higher profit
    
- Encourages producers to supply more
    

---

# ⚖ Market Equilibrium

When **quantity demanded = quantity supplied**, the market reaches **equilibrium price**.

## 🔹 Equilibrium Diagram

```
Price
  |
  |    /  (Supply)
  |   /
  |  /X  ← Equilibrium Point
  | /  \
  |/_____\________ Quantity
       (Demand)
```

At point **X**:

- No shortage
    
- No surplus

# 📘 Q1. Explain the Law of Demand. (5 Marks)

**Answer:**

The **Law of Demand** states that _other things remaining constant (ceteris paribus), when the price of a good increases, its quantity demanded decreases, and when the price decreases, quantity demanded increases._

### Assumptions:

1. Income remains constant
    
2. Tastes and preferences remain constant
    
3. Prices of related goods remain constant
    
4. No change in population
    

### Reasons:

- Law of diminishing marginal utility
    
- Income effect
    
- Substitution effect
    

Thus, the demand curve slopes downward from left to right.

---

# 📗 Q2. Explain the Law of Supply. (5 Marks)

**Answer:**

The **Law of Supply** states that _other things remaining constant, when the price of a good increases, its quantity supplied increases, and when price decreases, quantity supplied decreases._

### Assumptions:

1. Cost of production remains constant
    
2. Technology remains unchanged
    
3. No change in government policy
    
4. Number of sellers remains constant
    

### Reason:

- Profit motive (higher price encourages more production)
    

Thus, the supply curve slopes upward from left to right.

---

# ⚖ Q3. Explain Market Equilibrium. (5 Marks)

**Answer:**

Market equilibrium is a situation where **Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied** at a particular price.

### Equilibrium Price:

The price at which demand equals supply.

### Effects:

- No shortage (excess demand)
    
- No surplus (excess supply)
    
- Price becomes stable
    

If price is above equilibrium → Surplus occurs.  
If price is below equilibrium → Shortage occurs.

Hence, equilibrium ensures balance in the market.

# 📘 Q. Explain the Determinants of Demand and Supply. (5 Marks)

## 🔹 Determinants of Demand

Determinants of demand are factors that cause a **change in demand** (shift in demand curve).

Determinants of supply are factors that cause a **change in supply** (shift in supply curve).

1. **Price of the good** – Main factor affecting quantity supplied.
    
2. **Cost of production** – Higher cost reduces supply.
    
3. **Technology** – Better technology increases supply.
    
4. **Government policy** – Taxes reduce supply; subsidies increase supply.
    
5. **Prices of related goods** – Producers shift to more profitable goods.
    
6. **Number of sellers** – More sellers increase supply.

# **Circular Flow of Economy (5 Marks Answer)**

The circular flow of economy explains how money, goods, and services move continuously between households and firms in an economy. It shows the relationship between the goods market and the factor market.

There are four main parts in the circular flow model:

1. **Households** – Households are the owners of factors of production such as land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship. They provide these resources to firms and receive income in return in the form of wages, rent, interest, and profit.

2. **Firms** – Firms use the factors of production provided by households to produce goods and services. They sell these goods and services in the goods market.

3. **Factor Market** – In this market, households supply factors of production (labor, land, capital) to firms. In return, firms pay wages, rent, interest, and profit to households.

4. **Goods Market** – In this market, firms sell goods and services, and households purchase them by spending their income.

**Working of Circular Flow:**
Households provide resources to firms through the factor market and receive income. They then use this income to buy goods and services from firms in the goods market. Thus, goods flow from firms to households, and money flows from households to firms. This continuous movement forms a circular flow in the economy.

**Conclusion:**
Therefore, the circular flow diagram shows the continuous interaction between households and firms through the flow of goods, services, and money in an economy.
