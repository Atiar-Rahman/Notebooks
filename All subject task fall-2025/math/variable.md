
### **📘 Variables and Constants in Statistics**

---

### **🔹 What is a Variable?**

A **variable** is any **characteristic, number, or quantity that can change or vary** from one individual or observation to another.  
It is called “variable” because its value is **not fixed** — it can take **different values**.

**Examples:**

- Age of students in a class (16, 17, 18, …)
    
- Height, weight, income, temperature
    
- Gender (male, female, others)
    
- Exam marks of students
    

**👉 In short:**

> A variable is something that **can change or vary**.

---

### **📂 Types of Variables**

1. **Quantitative (Numerical) Variables:**
    
    - Represent **numbers or quantities**.
        
    - Example: height = 170 cm, weight = 65 kg, income = 20,000 BDT.
        
    - Two types:
        
        - **Discrete:** Whole numbers only (e.g., number of students = 30).
            
        - **Continuous:** Can take decimal values (e.g., height = 170.5 cm).
            
2. **Qualitative (Categorical) Variables:**
    
    - Represent **categories or qualities**, not numbers.
        
    - Example: gender (male/female), blood group (A, B, AB, O), color (red, blue).
        

---

### **🔹 What is a Constant?**

A **constant** is a **fixed value** that does **not change**.  
In statistics or mathematics, constants are used as reference or control values.

**Examples:**

- π (pi) = 3.1416
    
- Total number of days in a week = 7
    
- Gravity (g) = 9.8 m/s²
    
- If every student in a study gets the same textbook, then _textbook type_ is a constant.
    

**👉 In short:**

> A constant is something that **does not change**.

---

### **🧠 Key Difference Between Variable and Constant**

|Feature|Variable|Constant|
|---|---|---|
|**Definition**|A value that can change|A value that remains fixed|
|**Example**|Age, height, temperature|π = 3.1416, number of days in a week|
|**Change with observation?**|Yes|No|
|**Used for**|Representing data variation|Reference or fixed quantity|

---

## **📘 Different Types of Variables in Statistics**

In statistics, **variables** are the characteristics or properties that can **take different values** among individuals, objects, or observations.

Example:  
If you collect data about students, possible variables could be — _age_, _height_, _gender_, and _exam score_.

---

### **🔹 1. Quantitative (Numerical) Variables**

These variables **represent numbers** or **measurable quantities**.  
They show _how much_ or _how many_.

#### **a) Discrete Variables**

- Take **whole numbers only** (no decimals).
    
- Values are **countable**.
    
- Examples:
    
    - Number of students in a class (30, 31, 32…)
        
    - Number of cars owned
        
    - Number of books on a shelf
        

#### **b) Continuous Variables**

- Can take **any value**, including decimals or fractions.
    
- Values are **measurable**, not countable.
    
- Examples:
    
    - Height = 165.5 cm
        
    - Weight = 58.2 kg
        
    - Temperature = 36.7°C
        

**👉 In short:**

> Discrete → countable numbers  
> Continuous → measurable quantities

---

### **🔹 2. Qualitative (Categorical) Variables**

These variables **describe qualities or categories** rather than numbers.

#### **a) Nominal Variables**

- Represent **categories without any order**.
    
- Examples:
    
    - Gender (Male, Female, Other)
        
    - Blood group (A, B, AB, O)
        
    - Religion, eye color, city name
        

#### **b) Ordinal Variables**

- Represent **categories with a meaningful order or ranking**.
    
- Difference between ranks may not be equal.
    
- Examples:
    
    - Education level (Primary, Secondary, Higher)
        
    - Satisfaction (Poor, Fair, Good, Excellent)
        
    - Position in a race (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
        

---

### **🔹 3. Based on Relationship (Used in Research)**

#### **a) Independent Variable**

- The **cause** or factor that is changed or controlled in an experiment.
    
- Example: Type of fertilizer used on plants.
    

#### **b) Dependent Variable**

- The **effect** or outcome being measured.
    
- Example: Growth of the plant (depends on fertilizer type).
    

---

### **🧠 Summary Table**

|Type|Sub-type|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Quantitative**|Discrete|Countable numbers|Number of students|
||Continuous|Measurable values|Height, weight|
|**Qualitative**|Nominal|Categories without order|Gender, blood group|
||Ordinal|Ordered categories|Rank, satisfaction level|
|**Research Variables**|Independent|Cause or input|Type of fertilizer|
||Dependent|Effect or output|Plant growth|

## **📘 Differences Between Types of Variables**

Variables can be divided based on **data type** and **their role** in analysis or experiments.  
Let’s look at the main differences 👇

---

### **1️⃣ Quantitative vs Qualitative Variables**

|Feature|**Quantitative (Numerical)**|**Qualitative (Categorical)**|
|---|---|---|
|**Meaning**|Represent numbers or measurable quantities|Represent qualities or categories|
|**Values**|Expressed in numbers|Expressed in words or labels|
|**Examples**|Height, weight, age, income|Gender, color, religion, blood group|
|**Measurement**|Can be measured and compared numerically|Can only be classified or grouped|
|**Used for**|Calculations like mean, median, SD|Frequency or percentage analysis|

---

### **2️⃣ Discrete vs Continuous Variables (Both are Quantitative)**

|Feature|**Discrete Variable**|**Continuous Variable**|
|---|---|---|
|**Nature**|Countable|Measurable|
|**Possible Values**|Whole numbers only|Any value (including decimals)|
|**Examples**|Number of students, cars, books|Height, weight, temperature|
|**Data Form**|Finite or limited values|Infinite possible values|
|**Measurement Scale**|Usually interval or ratio|Always interval or ratio|

---

### **3️⃣ Nominal vs Ordinal Variables (Both are Qualitative)**

|Feature|**Nominal Variable**|**Ordinal Variable**|
|---|---|---|
|**Order**|No natural order or ranking|Has a meaningful order or ranking|
|**Examples**|Gender, blood group, religion|Education level, satisfaction rating|
|**Mathematical Meaning**|Numbers (if used) are just labels|Numbers show ranking but not equal distance|
|**Use**|To classify data|To rank or order data|

---

### **4️⃣ Independent vs Dependent Variables (Used in Research/Experiments)**

|Feature|**Independent Variable**|**Dependent Variable**|
|---|---|---|
|**Role**|The cause or factor that influences change|The effect or outcome being measured|
|**Control**|Controlled or changed by researcher|Changes depending on independent variable|
|**Examples**|Type of fertilizer used|Plant growth|
|**Other Names**|Input variable, predictor|Output variable, response|

---

### **🧠 Summary**

|Category|Subtypes|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Quantitative**|Discrete, Continuous|Number of students, Height|
|**Qualitative**|Nominal, Ordinal|Gender, Satisfaction level|
|**Experimental**|Independent, Dependent|Fertilizer type → Plant growth|
